Over the past nearly 8 years now (and in general, for all of us since learning became a regular part of our lives as children) its been a perpetual struggle to balance between thorough study preparation for exams, and, a general inquisitive nature driving discovery into a novel pursuit. Lately, I had been reflecting on how my personal studies have benefitted from approaches I developed for preparing for network industry certifications and I wanted to discuss this, at least briefly, as in my anecdotal experience it has been not just instrumental in passing exams but long-term retention of the knowledge imparted by these courses, contents, and ultimately their examinations.

I hope my photography skills help drive home the extent of my hours spent curled over coffee shops desks across the last decade. Between general course work, and the (pictured) industry certification flashcards I have spent many-an-hour with headphones on, a coffee to the side, and a lot, lot, lot of paper flying between pen and table. 

If this post has any intention its that I hope to at least encourage someone struggling with recall, and committing to memory the content of a course, of a new technical concept, to try written notes. Try not just written notes ( I certainly have plenty of notebooks filled with general, scratch notes from study as well ) but flashcards in particular. I would advocate, and I have to peers and fellow-students alike for most of my academic career now, that plenty of studies have shown the value of physically writing the ‘thing’ down. I feel that the textile nature of a physical card is not just more permanent in your hand than a digital note, quiz, or list of exam topics, but more likely to stick around into the future as you regularly hit those flashcards and commit topic > answer on the reverse side.

![](https://jamisonwireless.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/yup.jpg?w=459)

Back in 2021, when I first obtained my CCNA, I spent a very, very overwhelming amount of time preparing for that exam. Cisco exams are no joke! I should know, I’ve since been working at Cisco for 2+ years now on the wireless firmware for the Meraki MR product line and that stuff has some LAYERS! If I could plead anything of exam creators and question providers, please, please, a specific question about the intricacies of a command no engineer would ever use without first checking their resources should not account for 1/60 or any real chunk of a examinees score. The API documentation for a management solution or a Cisco IOS guidelines doc, or hell, these days even plugging the question/command into a AI chat model should be enough to get a “solid” answer. I would plead that we start writing questions which focus on critical thinking, and general knowledge of a subject more than intimate nonsense. I’m confident it will spawn better engineers and certification holders than “What path does this API call reference when cloning a configuration? home/org/conf, conf/org/clone, conf/… you get it. Its a “nothing-burger” of a question and only checks whether or not I’ve used that command 100 times to recall that path (spoiler, I haven’t), or, and heres the kicker, that I’ve made a flashcard for each of the API commands perhaps?

Unfortunately, we don’t yet live in a world where questions contributed to even top exams escape this trapping. Perhaps we never shall. Extensive, deeply-committed recollection of even niche, and at times in my opinion, nonsensically trivial detail is expected come exam day. While simulators for an exam like the CCNA or other networking certs go a long way, I would posit that above any other approach it was flashcards which best helped retain nuances of the Cisco IOS, or other solutions, in mind on exam day and beyond.

There’s nothing special to the study approach I’m expelling here. Its simply what works best for me and some others who struggle with general information retention in this age of ever-growing reliance, ironically, on the very technology we study and use to query about command formats. 😉 

If naught else then I hope readers will consider, if you will, the physical flash card as a tool for general study. 
If you have have any questions about my approach, and I haven’t yet written another post about how I structure my flashcards in more detail, please feel free to read out to me a the email associated with this blog, or on LinkedIn. 

Wishing you the best with your studies and, above all else, a 🎄Merry Christmas & Happy Holidays. 🎄
